# Professional Communication Guide
Professional communication is critical in the workplace. We use professional communication to apply for new jobs and internships, collaborate with colleagues, and build relationships with coworkers. Many students, especially those who are new to the workplace, are not aware of the unwritten rules of professional written and oral communication. This guide is meant to help you bridge that gap and ensure your communications are well-received by others in your office.

### General tips for professional communication

- Be clear and concise in your writing.
- Use a professional tone (no textspeak, emojis, or slang)
- Be respectful of other people's time. Respond to messages in a timely manner (ideally within 24 hours).

---

## 1\. Email Communications

#### Guidelines and structure for a professional email

- **Subject Line**
  - Choose a clear and specific phrase.
  - Do not leave the subject line blank.
  - Try to avoid generic subject lines like "Question" or "Meeting".
- **Greeting**
  - "Dear…" or "Hello…".
  - Use the person's preferred name and/or title.
  - If you are emailing a faculty member, refer to them as "Dr. \[Lastname\]" or "Professor \[Lastname\]". Never call a professor by their first name unless they have specifically requested it.
- **Opening Sentence**
  - Describe the purpose of your email.
  - Avoid sentences like "I hope this email finds you well."
- **Body**
  - Include key details, including specific question or need, any deadlines or meetings associated with the request, or any other details someone would need to understand your question.
  - Keep the email concise.
  - Review for typos and errors before sending.
  - Double-check any attachments needed on the email.
- **Closing**
  - Show appreciation and/or identify next steps.
- **Signature**
  - Sign off with a respectful closing phrase ("Sincerely", "Best", "Thank you", etc)
  - Include your name and contact details

### Example: Requesting a Meeting

**Subject:** Request for Meeting - Mentorship Check-In

**Email:**

Hello \[Name\],

I am writing to schedule our next mentorship meeting. I am available \[2-3 options of days and times\]. Please let me know what works best for you.

Thank you for your time!

Best,

\[Your Name\]

## 2\. Messaging (Slack, Teams, Chat)

#### How messaging is different from email

- Messages are generally shorter and more conversational than email, but still professional!
- Avoid sending messages that are too short or contain no context ("Hey!", "Can you help me?")
- Always include context and be patient waiting for a response ("I am working on \[X\]. Do you know where I can find the documentation for \[Y\] project?")

## 3\. Speaking in Professional Settings

- Expect to have to speak or present at work at least occasionally, in meetings, presentations, conferences, interviews, client meetings, etc.
- Try to organize your thoughts before speaking.
- When explaining your own (technical) work to others: avoid using too much jargon, so you do not lose your audience. Practice explaining what you did, why you did it, and what impact it had. Good scientists can explain complex ideas to a variety of audiences.

## 4\. Following Up on Communications

- Send a follow-up email shortly after any interview to say thank you to your interviewers. The email can be short, but you should always send one! After that, be patient. The job search process always takes longer than anyone thinks it will (including the search committee!). Assume it will be at least 2 more weeks than they expect, and be patient waiting for their response.
- If you are following up on an email waiting for a response, give the other person 2-3 days before you email again. Be respectful in your follow up email.

### Example: Thank You message

Hello \[Name\],

I would like to thank you for your time today discussing the \[role\] at \[company\]. I enjoyed learning more about the position and \[specific details you learned about the role/company during the conversation\]. I am excited about this position, and I am confident my background in \[1-2 skills\] makes me a great fit to contribute positively to your \[project/team/company\]. Please let me know if there is any other information I can provide as you make your decisions.

Thank you,

\[Your Name\]
