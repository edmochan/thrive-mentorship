# Student Networking Playbook
## 1\. What is Networking?

Networking is essentially having conversations with others that help you to grow and learn, typically centered around your career. Networking involves creating and building relationships, connecting with others over time, and learning from others' experiences. Networking is **not** just about getting something from someone you know. Do not use your network only when you need something! You will be most successful in networking when you are genuine and not transactional in your interactions with others.

### Types of Networking

#### Informational Networking

- Conducting short meetings or conversations with people who work in a given field
- Used to learn about working in a given field, industry trends, or even unadvertised opportunities in the field

#### Peer/Personal Networking

- Connecting with peers, classmates, and/or friends
- Used to exchange information, share advice, and develop soft skills

#### Social Networking

- Engaging in online communities to connect with peers and colleagues
- Used to share updates and milestones, meet others in your field, and demonstrate your expertise through written posts

#### Opportunity-Driven Networking

- Attending career-related events to advance your own career path (career fairs, conversations with recruiters, professional meet-ups, conferences, professional society meetings, etc)
- Used to learn more about specific job opportunities and to connect with others actively working in that field

---

## 2\. Why is Networking Important?

### Many opportunities come from connections

- Job openings are often promoted internally before they are posted externally.
- With a strong professional network who are aware of your goals and plans, you can learn about these jobs as well.

### It helps you learn things you didn't know about tech workplaces ("hidden curriculum")

- Learn more about how other companies run and what types of work styles you prefer. (In-person, remote, or hybrid? Predominantly individual contributions or a collaborative, team-based environment? Social events with your colleagues? Strictly 9-5 workday or flexible hours? There are many types of work environments, and different people thrive in different settings. Learn more about other places so you know what to strive for.)
- Discover what skills and technologies are most critical in other workplaces.
- Learn about the hiring process at other companies so you know what to expect in the future.

### Build your confidence as a professional in your field

- Become more comfortable talking about your personal and professional interests with people you do not know very well.
- Get feedback from others on your goals and career progression.

### Create a support system for yourself

- Mentors, peers, colleagues, faculty, and other professionals all become part of your network that you can lean on when you need to.
- The more people that know and care about you and your goals, the more likely it is that someone can help you to achieve them!

## 3\. How to Network Professionally

### Step 1: Clarify your own goals

- What roles am I hoping to get?
- Am I willing to move to get a job? Which cities/states would be feasible for me to move to?
- What more would I like to learn to help me get the job I want?

### Step 2: Identify people to connect with

- Every job fair, career event, or alumni event on campus is an opportunity to grow your network.
- Faculty, staff, friends, and peers can be part of your network.
- Coworkers and colleagues at your job or internship become part of your network.
- Your industry mentor is an important part of your network as well.
- Each person you connect with has their own network, and often you can be introduced to people through your existing network.
- Some people use LinkedIn to reach out to friends-of-friends or people in roles they may want to learn more about. If you are going to grow your network this way, consider reaching out to alumni of your school or early-career professionals, as they tend to be more likely to respond positively to your request.

### Step 3: Reach out to someone in your network

- Your goal for this conversation should be building a relationship, and not just using this person to get a job! Remember, genuine relationships pay off more than transactional ones.
- Send a clear and concise message requesting a meeting or conversation around your goals.
- Be respectful and specific in your message. Clarify what type of meeting you are seeking and be respectful of their time when making the request. Do not just ask for a job!
- Remember that getting a "no" is not a reflection on you! Not everyone has the time or ability to do a favor for someone in their network all the time.

#### Example: Requesting a Meeting

Hi \[Name\],

I am a student at Carlow University majoring in \[major\] and I am interested in \[job/company/role\]. Would you be willing to have a brief conversation about your experience and career path?

Thank you for your time!

Best,

\[Your Name\]

### Step 4: Prepare for the Meeting

- Review the person's job title, company, and professional background prior to the meeting. You don't need to memorize their entire resume, but you should be familiar with their work so you can ask intelligent questions about their career arc.
- Prepare a few (3 to 5) questions you would like to ask this person. Preparation will help the conversation flow more smoothly when you meet. Some suggestions of questions:
  - How did you get into this field?
  - What does a typical day look like in your role?
  - What skills or technologies are most important for me to focus on if I want to work in this field?
  - What do you wish you had known earlier?

### Step 5: Have the Meeting

- You might be nervous to talk to this person, especially if you don't know them well. That's okay! You did plenty of preparation before the meeting to help make things run smoothly. Remember, this person agreed to talk to you because they want to help you, not to judge you.
- Be engaged in the conversation. Do not wear headphones or spend time scrolling on your phone. Give this person your attention for as long as you are in the meeting.
- Listen attentively to their answers and be curious. Ask follow-up questions to help the conversation flow. Take notes if you wish.
- Expect that they will ask you some questions as well, and be open with your answers. They may ask things about the courses you have taken, jobs you have held, and your future goals. It is okay not to know everything, but answer what you can and ask for clarification if needed. Focus on learning, not impressing.
- End the meeting on time.
- Remember to thank them for their time at the end of the conversation!

### Step 6: Follow-Up Afterwards to Maintain the Connection

- Always follow up after a networking meeting. Send a message thanking them for their time.
- Stay connected to this person afterwards. Networking is an ongoing process, not a one-time meeting!
- Let this person know if you have a milestone to share (a new job, an internship, graduation, etc.) You can accomplish this through an update on LinkedIn or other social media sites, or through a personal message to the person.

#### Example: Follow-Up Message

Hi \[Name\],

Thank you for your time today! I really appreciated learning about \[job, career, role, etc\]. Your advice about \[specific detail\] was especially helpful.

Best,

\[Your Name\]

## 5\. Reflection Prompts

After a networking event, reflect on your conversation. Some things to consider:

- What did I learn from this conversation?
- What surprised me?
- How did this change my understanding of the field?
- What is my next step?

## 6\. Networking Checklist

Before reaching out:

☐ I know why I'm reaching out

☐ My message is clear and respectful

After the conversation:

☐ I sent a thank-you message

☐ I noted what I learned

☐ I plan to follow up later

☐ I reflected on what this taught me
