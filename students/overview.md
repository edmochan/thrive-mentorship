## 1\. Purpose of the Mentorship Experience

Welcome to the Carlow Industry Mentorship Program! This experience is designed to help you bridge your academic journey at Carlow with opportunities in industry, while supporting your growth as a whole person.

Mentorship has so many benefits for students, including:

- Expanding your exposure to professional pathways
- Providing access to industry knowledge and networks
- Introducing you to careers and roles that you may not otherwise know about
- Increasing your confidence and sense of belonging in tech
- Learning things not traditionally taught in a computer science class
- Helping you navigate areas that are often not explicitly taught (sometimes call the "hidden curriculum")

Remember, mentorship provides a partnership with a professional in your field of interest, focusing on your individual growth as a future professional in that field. Mentorship is **not** a passive experience; you must be actively engaged in the partnership to get the most out of it. Expect to be leading the conversations with your mentor, so they can provide you with the information and insights that you would find most useful.

Your mentor can help you with things like resumes, cover letters, applications, interviewing, and other career-related skills as well. This mentorship program is not a guarantee for a job or internship, but their advice can be invaluable in helping you find the position you are ultimately looking for.

Below is an overview of the mentorship program, along with links to various resources provided. 

## 2\. What You Gain from this Experience

## 3\. Your Role as a Mentee

This program is most valuable when you **actively and respectfully engage** in the process. We want you to be committed, curious, and connected. As a participant in this program, you are expected to:

### A. Take Ownership

- Set goals for yourself and revisit them regularly. If you aren't reaching your goals, think about why.
  - Were your goals too ambitious?
  - Do you need to engage more readily to reach them?
  - Discuss with your faculty advisor or your mentor if you need help!
- Follow through on agreed-upon tasks. Treat this relationship professionally.

### B. Come Prepared

- Bring questions, updates, or topics to each meeting. Remember, you set the agenda and you drive the conversations! Do not expect your mentor to be responsible for determining what you want to talk about.
- Reflect on previous conversations and think about what you may want to follow up on.

### C. Communicate Professionally

- Respond to messages in a timely manner. Do not ignore emails and/or ghost your mentor.
- Be respectful and clear in communication. Do not write your emails like you write your texts!
- Notify your mentor in advance if you need to reschedule. Do not skip meetings without notice!

### D. Practice Self-Advocacy

- Ask questions when you don't understand something. It is okay to be unfamiliar with things your mentor mentions! Asking questions helps you both get more from the conversation.
- Share your goals, concerns, and interests openly. Be open to new ideas and approaches.

Your mentor will probably make suggestions to you about internships, career paths, courses, or other topics on which they consider you may need advice. You are not obligated to act on all of these suggestions, but part of mentorship will be an openness to new ideas. If you aren't sure about something, ask your faculty advisor for a second opinion.

We have provided templates for you to track your progress towards your goals, and to take notes and reflect on your meetings with your mentor. While these sheets are not required, they may help you to keep track of your conversations and provide reflections to discuss with your mentor at your next meeting.

## 4\. The Role of an Industry Mentor

Your mentor can:

- Share experiences and insights about industry
- Help you understand professional expectations
- Provide feedback on resumes, interviews, and communication
- Support your growth and confidence
- Connect you with their professional network
- Share openings for job shadowing, internships, or other out-of-the-classroom learning opportunities

**It is important to remember that your industry mentor does not replace your faculty (academic) advisor!** You should always work with your advisor on things like course scheduling, choosing electives, assignment help, problems in courses, or any other academic issue. Your faculty advisor can also help you with career planning and feedback on your resume. Your mentor serves as another point-of-view on career-related issues.

## 5\. Making the Most of Your Meetings

**Before the Meeting**

- Reflect: What did you learn last time? What do you still have questions about?
- Prepare 2-3 questions or topics to discuss

**During the Meeting**

- Be present and engaged
- Take notes
- Ask follow-up questions

**After the Meeting**

- Review key takeaways
- Complete any action items
- If possible, apply what you learned and reflect on how it goes

## 6\. Example Questions You Can Ask

**Career Exploration**

- What does a typical day in your role look like?
- What skills are most important in your field?
- How did you decide what you wanted to do with your career?
- What is the typical work-life balance like in this field?
- How has AI affected your job/field?

**Skill Development**

- What should I focus on improving right now?
- How can I strengthen my resume or portfolio?
- What courses or skills do you wish you had learned in college?
- What are some skills that an entry-level job applicant in your field would need to have?

**Hidden Curriculum**

- What do interns or new employees often misunderstand about the workplace?
- What are expectations that aren't usually explained?
- What advice do you wish you had received early in your career?
- What is the culture like at your workplace, and how does it differ from other places you have worked?
- What was the application and interview process like for your job?

**Professional Growth**

- What helped you succeed early in your career?
- What mistakes should I try to avoid?
- Do many people in your field go on to graduate work, or is a bachelor's degree sufficient for this work?

## 7\. Common Challenges (and How to Navigate Them)

**"I don't know what to ask."**

- Start with your current experiences (classes, projects). Your mentor wants to know more about you!
- Ask about your mentor's journey.

**"I feel intimidated."**

- Remember: your mentor is here to support you, not to judge you.
- It's okay to not know everything. If you did, you wouldn't be in this program!

**"I'm not seeing progress."**

- Revisit your goals
- Ask for more structured feedback

**"I'm not connecting well with my mentor."**

- Talk to the program coordinator.
- We can make adjustments to the mentor-mentee pairs if needed.

**"My mentor isn't answering the questions I have."**

- Consider rephrasing your questions. Your mentor may be misunderstanding the purpose of your questions.
- Review your notes from the meetings and look for places where you can more clearly express your goals.

**"I'm having trouble finding time to meet with my mentor."**

- Your mentor is doing you a favor volunteering to work with you. Be flexible with your schedule to try to accommodate times that work for your mentor as much as possible.
- If you are still having trouble, reach out to teh program coordinator for assistance.

## 8\. Carlow Values in Mentorship

Your participation in this program reflects Carlow's commitment to Mercy values. These values should guide your engagement in creating a strong and respectful relationship with your mentor. Remember that you are representing Carlow when you are interacting with your mentor. We expect you to keep Carlow's mission and values in mind as you navigate this relationship.

**Hospitality**

- Be open, respectful, and engaged in conversations
- Create a welcoming and positive interaction with your mentor

**Service**

- Approach the relationship with gratitude and responsibility
- Show up prepared and respectful of your mentor's time

**Discovery**

- Be curious and willing to explore new ideas, paths, and perspectives
- Ask questions-even if they feel basic

**Sacredness of Creation**

- Honor your own growth and identity
- Respect your mentor's experiences and perspectives
- Respect your mentor's time - do not skip meetings or ignore emails from your mentor!

**Mercy**

- Recognize and reflect on issues of access, equity, and inclusion
- Engage thoughtfully in conversations about barriers and opportunities
